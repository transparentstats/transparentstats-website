The current draft of the transparent statistics guidelines has an FAQ and exemplar on effect sizes. During a meeting we ran at CHI to collect feedback, a participant strongly objected to our use of the term â€œeffect sizeâ€ in the guidelines. We should report effect sizes. But what are effect sizes?

We are repeatedly told that effect sizes are important, and many methodologists urge us to report effect sizes. For example, Ron Wasserstein, the executive director of the American Statistical Association, stated that:

In the post p<0.05 era, scientific argumentation is not based on whether a p-value is small enough or not. Attention is paid to effect sizes and confidence intervals. Evidence is thought of as being continuous rather than some sort of dichotomy. (McCook 2016)

Unfortunately, methodologists rarely explain what they exactly mean by â€œeffect sizeâ€. The current draft of the transparent statistics guidelines (section 2.1, Effect Size FAQ) mentions that there is a narrow sense and a broad sense of the term â€œeffect sizeâ€. Briefly, the narrow sense refers to a family of standardized (i.e., unitless) measures such as Cohenâ€™s d, while the broad sense refers to any measure of interest, standardized or not. This includes simple and familiar metrics like unstandardized mean differences, e.g., a mean difference in completion time between two techniques, expressed in seconds.

The guidelines currently use â€œeffect sizeâ€ in a broad sense, and often mentions unstandardized mean differences as an example. At the CHI meeting, a participant who was manifestly knowledgeable about effect sizes strongly objected to this. If I recall correctly, the reasons were: 1) statisticians do not use the term â€œeffect sizeâ€ in that broad sense, 2) unstandardized mean differences are problematic, and calling them â€œeffect sizesâ€ may encourage HCI researchers to abuse them. There are two distinct questions here:

  1. Is it appropriate to use the term â€œeffect sizeâ€ to mean things like unstandardized mean differences?
  2. Which measure of effect size should be reported?

Both questions are important, but in this post, I will only focus on a).

Jacob Cohen is a renown statistician and psychologist who contributed to laying the foundations of meta-analysis. His definition was broad and clearly included unstandardized mean differences:

Why the different meanings today?

Iâ€™m far from having done a complete literature survey and I can only speculate. But it seems possible that:

  1. When he formalized meta-analysis, Jacob Cohen introduced the term â€œeffect sizeâ€ that he unambiguously meant in a broad sense. He however focused his research on standardized measures of effect sizes, which he referred to as â€œindices of effect sizeâ€.
  2. Subsequent researchers writing about meta-analysis came to use the term â€œeffect sizeâ€ as a synonym for those â€œinterestingâ€ (for meta-analysis) types of effect size, and perhaps ended up forgetting Cohenâ€™s original definition.
  3. More recently, methodologists focusing on statistical communication (rather than meta-analysis) started to employ the term â€œeffect sizeâ€ with a similar meaning to Cohenâ€™s original definition, albeit stripped from its reference to statistical significance.

Two methodologists, Ken Kelley and Kristopher Preacher, provide a good overview of the different meanings of â€œeffect sizeâ€ used in the literature since Jacob Cohen (Preacher and Kelley 2011; Kelley and Preacher 2012). They conclude that a broad definition of effect size is the most useful:

In response to the need for a general, inclusive definition of effect size, we define effect size as any measure that reflects a quantity of interest, either in an absolute sense or as compared with some specified value. The quantity of interest might refer to variability, association, difference, odds, rate, duration, discrepancy, proportionality, superiority, or degree of fit or misfit. [â€¦] Although standardized effect sizes can be valuable, they are not always to be preferred over an effect size that is wedded to the original measurement scale, which may already be expressed in meaningful units that appropriately address the question of interest. Their definition clearly includes unstandardized mean differences:

For example, group mean differences in scores on a widely understood instrument for measuring depressive symptoms are already expressed on a metric that is understandable to depression researchers, and to standardize effects involving the scale would only confuse matters. (Preacher and Kelley 2011, 95)

We have seen several methodologists who argue for adopting a broad definition of the term â€œeffect sizeâ€. Meanwhile, I havenâ€™t seen papers where a methodologist explicitly argues that the term â€œeffect sizeâ€ should be used in a narrow sense. In all the papers Iâ€™ve seen, the narrow definition seems to be used in a stipulative manner, without authors giving much thought to it.

So how should we use the term?

From all this, it seems clear that â€œeffect sizeâ€ can be used both in a broad sense and in a narrow sense, provided that the author is consistent and clarifies what they are talking about.

In my opinion, the broad sense is more useful in general methodological discussions. The terms â€œunstandardized (or simple) effect sizeâ€ and â€œstandardized effect sizeâ€ are widely used and perfectly capable of resolving any ambiguity. Using â€œeffect sizeâ€ in a broad sense doesnâ€™t commit anyone to any particular statistical reporting practice. When the truth of a statement depends on whether it refers to standardized or unstandardized effect sizes, the term â€œeffect sizeâ€ without a qualifier should simply be avoided.

Using â€œeffect sizeâ€ to mean only â€œstandardized effect sizeâ€ is problematic because it requires finding another term for unstandardized effect sizes. I donâ€™t recall any user of the narrow definition proposing such a term, and even if they did, a different term could give the wrong impression that unstandardized and standardized effect sizes have nothing in common. Cohen and others were clear about the analogies between the two.

I referred to â€œtheâ€ broad sense" and â€œtheâ€ narrow sense, but in reality there are many possible definitions of â€œeffect sizeâ€ in both cases. If one needs an explicit definition, the one from Preacher and Kelley (2011) quoted above is quite extensive and informed by a good knowledge of the past literature. Effect size is defined as a quantitative reflection of the magnitude of some phenomenon that is used for the purpose of addressing a question of interest. [â€¦] The question of interest might refer to central tendency, variability, association, difference, odds, rate, duration, discrepancy, proportionality, superiority, or degree of fit or misfit, among others. Statistical terminology is notoriously messy (Grace-Martin 2018). The case of effect sizes is one among many where blanket statements containing the word â€œstatisticiansâ€ should be taken with skepticism (Gelman 2018). Few HCI researchers appreciate the number of statistics topics on which there is no consensus. When there is no consensus on a topic, article authors should be free to choose the option they prefer, provided that their choice is made explicit and justified with references from the methodology literature. It is bad behavior for a reviewer to ignore these references and force their own opinion on the authors. As HCI Professor Shumin Zhai put it, â€œresearch results, paper writing, and reviewing are just not the right forum for statistical method discussion.â€ (Robertson and Kaptein 2016, v).
